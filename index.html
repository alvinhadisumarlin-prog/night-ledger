I.

The Bestiary

MY-001

Pontianak

also Kuntilanak · the woman who died in childbirth

Malay / Nusantara Vengeful spirit SG · MY · ID ◆◆◆◆◇

She announces herself by scent before sight — frangipani, sweet and too heavy, the smell of a grave dressed in flowers. They say a woman who dies in childbirth, or carrying a child, returns this way: beautiful from the front in a white dress with hair to the waist, ruined from behind, a hollow in her back.

The cry gives the rule that keeps you alive. When her wail sounds faint and far, she is close. When it is loud, she has already passed you by. She is drawn to the smell of laundry hung out overnight and to new fathers, and she is said to nest in the banana trees, which is why some still tie a red thread around the trunk.

Keeper's note

The old ward is a nail or sharp iron driven into the hollow at the nape of her neck — do this and she is said to become a dutiful wife until the nail is ever pulled free. Bukit Brown and the older kampong land carry the most sightings.

MY-002

Penanggalan

the detaching head · cousin to the Thai Krasue

Malay / Nusantara Detaching revenant MY · TH · ID ◆◆◆◆◆

By day she is an ordinary woman, often a midwife or a beauty who bargained for her looks. By night her head lifts free of her shoulders and rises into the dark, dragging her stomach and entrails beneath it like a lantern of wet rope, faintly glowing as it drifts over the rooftops.

She hunts the blood of women in labour and of newborns, settling on the roof above a birthing room to drink through unseen seams. Before dawn she soaks her trailing organs in vinegar so they shrink enough to fold back inside her sleeping body — which is the one moment she can be undone.

Keeper's note

Households would lay the thorny leaves of the mengkuang or jeruju at the windows; the trailing entrails snag, and she is held until light. Scatter glass or thorns on the roof and she cannot land. A near-identical creature flies the rice fields of Thailand as the Krasue.

MY-003

Toyol

the spirit child · the little thief

Malay / Nusantara Bound familiar SG · MY · ID ◆◆◇◇◇

A Toyol is a small green-skinned infant spirit, eyes wide and red, raised from a dead fetus by a bomoh and kept in a jar. Its keeper feeds it drops of blood from a pricked toe and sends it out at night to steal — money, gold, small valuables lifted from sleeping households who never feel a thing.

It has the mind of a toddler, easily delighted and easily fooled, which is its only weakness. A house that suspects a Toyol will leave out beads, marbles, or sand by the door; the creature stops to count and play and forgets why it came, and is gone by morning.

Keeper's note

Sudden, unexplained good fortune in a neighbour was the classic accusation. Money kept beside a mirror or a needle is said to repel it. When the keeper dies, the Toyol must be passed on or it torments the family — which is how the jars travel.

MY-004

Pocong

the shroud ghost · the bound dead

Malay / Nusantara Restless dead ID · MY · SG ◆◆◆◇◇

In Muslim burial the body is wrapped in white cloth, the kain kafan, tied at the crown of the head, under the chin, and at the feet. The ties are meant to be loosened in the grave. If they are forgotten, the soul cannot leave — and after forty days it rises still bound, a face peering from a knot of grave-cloth, moving the only way it can: in slow, jolting hops.

It is not malicious so much as desperate. The Pocong wants only release, and it returns to those who knew it, asking without words to have the binding at last untied.

Keeper's note

To free it, the head-tie must be undone — then it rests. Encountered along quiet roads and near fresh graves. The horror is less in malice than in the helplessness of a thing trapped at the threshold and unable to cross.

CN-001

Jiangshi

殭屍 · the hopping corpse

Chinese Reanimated dead CN · SG diaspora ◆◆◆◇◇

Stiff with rigor and dressed in the robes of a Qing official, the Jiangshi cannot bend its limbs, so it moves in rigid hops, arms outstretched, hunting the warm breath and life-essence of the living. It is a corpse that failed to rest — improperly buried, dead far from home, or stirred by foul energy.

It is blind and tracks you by your breathing alone. The old advice is the strangest and the simplest: stand still, and hold your breath until it passes. A Taoist priest seals it with a strip of yellow paper inscribed in cinnabar, pressed to its brow, and it freezes mid-hop.

Keeper's note

Folk wards include mirrors (which terrify it), sticky rice, the wood of a peach tree, and a threshold board high enough that stiff legs cannot clear it. The legend may grow from the real trade of "transporting a corpse home" over long roads in old China.

MY-005

Orang Minyak

the oily man

Malay / Nusantara Cursed man MY · SG ◆◆◇◇◇

A figure slick with black oil from head to foot, naked and gleaming, who slips through windows at night to prey on young women. The oil is the point of him: grab hold and your hands slide off, so he can never be caught or held.

The tellings split on what he is. Some say a man who made a dark pact for power and must take a quota of victims to keep it; others, a living criminal who smears himself in oil precisely so no one can seize him, and lets the legend do his hiding for him. In 1960s Singapore and Malaysia the panics were real enough to empty streets after dark.

Keeper's note

One of the more "human-shaped" entries — interesting precisely because it sits on the seam between folklore and ordinary crime, the supernatural story a community tells to explain a frightening, very real prowler.

II.

Haunted Ground — Singapore

SG-001

Old Changi Hospital

the wards on the hill

Singapore site Haunted place Changi ◆◆◆◆◇

Built by the British in the 1930s, the hilltop complex became, during the Japanese Occupation, a site associated with the Kempeitai and with the suffering of those held and interrogated there. Long after it closed as a hospital, it kept its reputation as one of the most haunted places on the island.

The accounts repeat the same notes: cold pockets in warm corridors, the cries of children in empty wards, a nurse seen drifting where no one walks, faces at the windows photographed from outside. Trespassers describe the building seeming to wake around them — the sense of being counted, and found.

Keeper's note

Distinguish layers here: documented wartime history, decades of trespasser lore, and the embellishment of ghost-tour retellings. The strongest entries note which layer each claim belongs to.

SG-002

Pulau Tekong

the island of recruits · "Charlie Company"

Singapore site Military legend Pulau Tekong ◆◆◆◆◆

Every cohort of conscripts inherits the island's stories before they ever set foot on it. The most enduring: a company of recruits — Charlie Company, the tellings agree — that vanished on a night exercise and was quietly struck from the records, so that the bunk you sleep in may be one no roll call admits exists.

Then the smaller, surer tales: the extra pair of boots marching in the dark, the section that counts one too many heads, the figure standing at the foot of the bed at 3am, the soldier who hears his own name called from the treeline in a voice he knows is not human.

Keeper's note

A rare living tradition — passed mouth to mouth through national service, mutating each intake. Worth collecting first-hand: the same core motifs surface independently from men who never met, which is its own kind of unsettling.

SG-003

Matilda House

Istana Woodneuk's quieter cousin · the Punggol mansion

Singapore site Cursed house Punggol ◆◆◆◇◇

A colonial bungalow built in the 1900s, named for a man's late wife, it stood derelict in Punggol for decades while the district emptied and then rebuilt around it. The legend holds that a son cursed the house — or that a grieving mother did — so that no one who tried to move in would ever stay, doors slamming, sleep broken, the living driven out.

What makes it linger is its survival: the conserved facade now sits inside a modern condominium, the old dread folded into a show-flat, the past kept like a tooth set in new gold.

Keeper's note

A good case study in how a legend adapts to redevelopment rather than dying with the building. Track how the story is retold now that the house is a gatehouse for flats.
